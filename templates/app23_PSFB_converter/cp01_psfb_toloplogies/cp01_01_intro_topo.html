Center-Tapped vs Current Doubler Topologies

Center-tapped and Current doubler are two classic full-wave rectification topologies (using two diodes or synchronous rectifiers) commonly used at the secondary side of isolated DC-DC converters, especially in switched-mode power supplies (SMPS) such as phase-shifted full-bridge, LLC, forward converters, etc.

1. Center-Tapped (with center tap)

Schematic overview

  • Secondary winding of the transformer has a center tap connected to ground (or output reference).
  • Two diodes (or synchronous MOSFETs) connected to the two ends of the secondary.
  • One output inductor + output filter capacitor after the diodes.

How it works

  • In each half-cycle, only one diode conducts (when the voltage is positive at one end).
  • Output inductor current is continuous, but each diode/MOSFET carries current only half the time.
  • Output ripple frequency = 2 × switching frequency (that's why fc/2 appears in some parameters).

Advantages

  • Very simple structure, few magnetic components on secondary (only one output inductor).
  • Only two active devices (diodes or MOSFETs).

Major disadvantages

  • Poor transformer utilization: only half of the secondary winding is active in each half-cycle.
  • High RMS current through diodes (≈ Is/√2).
  • Center tap required → difficult and mechanically fragile at high currents.
  • High diode conduction losses unless synchronous rectification is used.

2. Current Doubler

Schematic overview

  • Transformer secondary has no center tap → single simple winding.
  • Two diodes (or synchronous MOSFETs) + two output inductors (L1 and L2) connected in parallel to the output.
  • Inductors can be separate or magnetically coupled, operating in interleaved mode.

How it works

  • When transformer delivers positive voltage → one diode conducts, current ramps up in L1.
  • When transformer delivers negative voltage → other diode conducts, current ramps up in L2.
  • Both inductors deliver current alternately to the load → "current doubling" effect (hence the name).
  • Output ripple frequency = switching frequency (fc), not fc/2.

Advantages

  • Simple secondary transformer (no center tap) → much easier to wind and handle at high currents / low voltages.
  • Each inductor carries only half the output current → smaller, cheaper inductors.
  • Similar or lower RMS current through diodes/MOSFETs.
  • Better transformer utilization (entire secondary winding active in both half-cycles).
  • Lower ripple on output capacitor.

Disadvantages

  • Two output inductors instead of one → more magnetic components.
  • Higher RMS current through transformer secondary (≈ Is instead of Is/2).

When to choose which?

  • Center-tapped → when you want maximum simplicity and have high output voltage (fewer secondary turns).
  • Current doubler → when you have high output current, low voltage, and want a more efficient transformer + smaller inductors.
