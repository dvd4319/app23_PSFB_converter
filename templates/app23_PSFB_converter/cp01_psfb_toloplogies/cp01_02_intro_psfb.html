Why a Current Doubler Secondary Was Selected

For a high-power, isolated DC-DC converter based on a Phase-Shift Full Bridge (PSFB) topology, the primary side architecture is largely fixed, while the secondary side offers significant flexibility. This design freedom on the secondary side allows optimization for efficiency, current handling, and thermal performance.

Primary Side Considerations

In a PSFB converter, the primary side topology is well established and changes very little between designs:

  • Full-bridge configuration using four power switches
  • Control via phase-shift between the two bridge legs
  • Nearly constant switching frequency
  • Zero Voltage Switching (ZVS) achieved using transformer leakage inductance and device output capacitances

Although component choices may vary (Si or SiC MOSFETs, gate drivers, snubber networks), these variations do not alter the fundamental behavior of the primary side. As a result, optimization efforts are mainly focused on the secondary side.

Secondary Side Design Flexibility

The secondary side topology can be selected based on application-specific requirements such as:

  • Output voltage level
  • Output current magnitude
  • Efficiency targets
  • Cost constraints
  • Design complexity

Common Secondary Options

Diode Rectification

Simple diode-based rectification (center-tap or bridge) is generally unsuitable for low-voltage, high-current applications. At 12 V and currents above 200 A, conduction losses become excessive, making this option impractical for automotive use.

Synchronous Rectification

Replacing diodes with MOSFETs significantly reduces conduction losses and is widely used in low-voltage converters. This approach is common and often serves as the foundation for more advanced secondary structures.

Current Doubler Rectifier

The current doubler topology builds on synchronous rectification and introduces two output inductors to split the load current between two paths. This structure offers several key advantages:

  • Lower output current ripple
  • Reduced RMS current in each inductor and MOSFET
  • Lower conduction and thermal stress on secondary-side components
  • Improved efficiency at very high output currents

These characteristics make the current doubler particularly well suited for 12 V outputs at power levels around 3 kW and above, where output currents exceed 200 A.

Active Secondary Bridge

Fully active secondary bridges (similar to a dual-active-bridge structure) increase control flexibility but add significant complexity and cost. For a unidirectional 12 V automotive DC-DC converter, this solution is rarely justified.

Transformer Considerations

Although the primary topology remains fixed, the transformer design is closely linked to the chosen secondary structure. Parameters such as turns ratio, leakage inductance, magnetizing inductance, and winding arrangement are optimized to support the current doubler operation and ensure high efficiency and reliable ZVS behavior.

Conclusion

For a high-power automotive DC-DC converter with a 12 V output:

  • The PSFB primary side is a mature and largely fixed solution
  • The secondary side provides the main opportunity for optimization
  • The current doubler topology offers the best balance of efficiency, current handling, and thermal performance

As a result, the combination of a PSFB primary with synchronous rectification and a current doubler secondary is a well-established and proven choice in automotive applications.
